Bates V. State Bar of Arizona
433 U.S. 350 (1977)Appellants (Bates & O'Steen) were censured by the Arizona Supreme Court for advertising their legal services. They appealed, challenging the disciplinary sanction under the First Amendment and Sherman Act. | Us Supreme Court | 1977-06-27
Facts

Two lawyers formed a legal clinic offering low-cost services to moderate-income clients. To attract clients, they placed a newspaper ad listing their fees for routine services (uncontested divorces, bankruptcies, adoptions, etc.). The Arizona State Bar initiated disciplinary proceedings.

Procedural History

Appellants (Bates & O'Steen) were sanctioned (suspension) by the State Bar of Arizona for advertising their legal services. They appealed, challenging the disciplinary sanction under the First Amendment and Sherman Act.
The Arizona Supreme Court reviewed and reduced the sanction to censure only, rejecting their First Amendment and Sherman Act claims. The U.S. Supreme Court granted certiorari.

Issues

Does the First Amendment protect advertisements by lawyers?

Holding

Yes, the first amendment does allow lawyers to advertise in a manner that is not misleading to the general public

Rule of Law

Commercial speech is protected under the First Amendment when it proposes a commercial transaction. Lawyer advertising that is not false, deceptive, or misleading is entitled to First Amendment protection.

Reasoning

The Court reasoned that:
(1) Speech proposing a commercial transaction does not lose First Amendment protection merely because it is commercial;
(2) Consumers have a substantial interest in receiving information about legal services, including price information;
(3) The traditional ban on lawyer advertising was an antiquated rule of etiquette that created higher barriers to entry and perpetuated the market position of established attorneys;
(4) Advertising for routine legal services is not inherently misleading since such services can be meaningfully priced—the State Bar itself sponsored a program with standardized rates;
(5) States may still ban false, deceptive, or misleading advertising and may require disclaimers.

Concurring Opinion

Burger (concurring): Agreed but would have allowed more restrictions
Powell (concurring in part): Worried consumers couldn't assess the "diagnosis and advice" value of legal services

Dissenting Opinion

Rehnquist (dissenting in part): Would have found the advertising more problematic

Analysis & Significance

This case effectively ended the ban on lawyer advertisements and for better or worse, opened the door to modern legal service market and ads.
