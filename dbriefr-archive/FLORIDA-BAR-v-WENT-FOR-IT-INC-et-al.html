FLORIDA BAR v. WENT FOR IT, INC., et al.
515 U.S. 618 | Supreme Court of the United States | 1995-06-21
Facts

In 1989, the Florida Bar completed a two-year study of lawyer advertising effects and proposed amendments to its advertising rules. In late 1990, the Florida Supreme Court adopted amendments including Rule 4-7.4(b)(1), prohibiting lawyers from sending targeted direct-mail solicitations to accident victims or their relatives for 30 days following an accident or disaster, and Rule 4-7.8(a), prohibiting lawyer referral services from making similar contacts.

In March 1992, attorney G. Stewart McHenry and his referral service, Went For It, Inc., filed suit in federal district court challenging these rules as violations of the First and Fourteenth Amendments. McHenry alleged he routinely sent targeted solicitations to accident victims within 30 days and wished to continue. Went For It, Inc. sought to contact accident victims within 30 days and refer them to participating lawyers. McHenry was disbarred in October 1992 for unrelated reasons; attorney John T. Blakely was substituted.

The District Court granted summary judgment for plaintiffs. The Eleventh Circuit affirmed. The Florida Bar appealed to the U.S. Supreme Court, which granted certiorari.

Procedural History

In March 1992, G. Stewart McHenry and Went For It, Inc. filed suit in the United States District Court for the Middle District of Florida seeking declaratory and injunctive relief challenging two Florida Bar advertising rules as unconstitutional. A Magistrate Judge recommended granting summary judgment for the Bar, finding the rules constitutional. The District Court rejected this recommendation and entered summary judgment for the plaintiffs in October 1992. The Eleventh Circuit Court of Appeals affirmed. The U.S. Supreme Court granted certiorari and reversed the lower courts' decisions.

Issues

Whether Florida Bar Rules prohibiting targeted direct-mail solicitations to accident victims and their relatives for 30 days following an accident or disaster violate the First and Fourteenth Amendments.

Holding

The Court reversed the Court of Appeals and held that Florida's Rule 4-7.4(b)(1), which prohibits lawyers from sending targeted direct-mail solicitations to accident victims and their relatives for 30 days following an accident or disaster, does not violate the First and Fourteenth Amendments. The Rule satisfies the three-pronged Central Hudson test for commercial speech restrictions: the State has a substantial interest in protecting victims' privacy and the legal profession's reputation; the restriction directly and materially advances that interest based on empirical evidence; and the 30-day ban is narrowly tailored to achieve the desired objective.

Rule of Law

States may restrict targeted direct-mail solicitations by lawyers to accident victims and their relatives for 30 days following an accident or disaster without violating the First Amendment. Under the Central Hudson test for commercial speech, such restrictions are constitutional when: (1) the state asserts a substantial interest in protecting the privacy and tranquility of injured persons and preserving public confidence in the legal profession; (2) the regulation directly and materially advances that interest, supported by empirical evidence and anecdotal data demonstrating real harm; and (3) the restriction is narrowly tailored to achieve the desired objective, requiring a reasonable fit between ends and means—not the least restrictive means. The temporal limitation and availability of alternative advertising channels satisfy this standard. The restriction applies to all accident victims regardless of injury severity and does not require distinguishing between degrees of harm.
