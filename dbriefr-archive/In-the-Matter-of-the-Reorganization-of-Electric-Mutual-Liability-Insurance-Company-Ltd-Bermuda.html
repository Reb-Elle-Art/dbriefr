In the Matter of the Reorganization of Electric Mutual Liability Insurance Company Ltd. (Bermuda)
425 Mass. 419 | Massachusetts Supreme Judicial Court | 1997-07-11
Facts

Electric Mutual Liability Insurance Company (EMLICO) applied to the Commissioner of Insurance to reorganize and redomesticate to Bermuda. Allstate Insurance Company, Kemper Reinsurance Company, and other reinsurance companies sought judicial review of the Commissioner's approval. During the Superior Court proceedings, counsel for Allstate received two documents from an anonymous source that the reinsurers claimed showed EMLICO had misrepresented its financial condition to the Commissioner. A Superior Court judge found any attorney-client privilege had been waived. EMLICO appealed; a single justice of the Appeals Court imposed conditions on document use but allowed broad disclosure in related proceedings and litigation. When Kemper received a federal grand jury subpoena for the documents, the single justice referred to an Appeals Court panel the question of whether Kemper could produce them despite EMLICO's privilege claims. The case reached the Supreme Judicial Court on interlocutory appeal. Subsequently, the underlying Superior Court actions were dismissed.

Procedural History

Reinsurance companies sought judicial review in Superior Court of the Commissioner of Insurance's approval of EMLICO's reorganization and redomestication to Bermuda. A Superior Court judge ruled that attorney-client privilege over two disputed documents had been waived. EMLICO appealed to a single justice of the Appeals Court, who denied relief but imposed conditions on document use. When a federal grand jury subpoenaed the documents, the single justice referred the privilege question to an Appeals Court panel. The Massachusetts Supreme Judicial Court transferred the case on its own motion. The Superior Court actions were subsequently dismissed, rendering the interlocutory appeal moot.

Issues

Whether an anonymous disclosure of otherwise privileged documents mandates a waiver of attorney-client privilege when the privilege holder took reasonable precautions to maintain confidentiality.

Holding

The appeal is dismissed as moot because the underlying Superior Court actions were dismissed during pendency of the interlocutory appeal. However, the court establishes that disclosure of privileged documents by an anonymous source does not automatically constitute waiver of attorney-client privilege. A client may establish privilege exists and no waiver occurred by demonstrating reasonable precautionary steps were taken to ensure confidentiality, shifting the presumption against voluntary disclosure.

Rule of Law

The attorney-client privilege is strictly construed, and the party asserting the privilege bears the burden of proving: (1) communications were received from a client seeking legal advice; (2) communications were made in confidence; and (3) the privilege has not been waived. Under the modern trend, disclosure of a privileged document by an anonymous source does not automatically constitute waiver. Rather, if a client demonstrates reasonable precautionary steps were taken to ensure confidentiality, the presumption arises that disclosure was involuntary and no waiver occurred. Only a client can waive the privilege, and waiver requires voluntary disclosure or consent to disclosure. Reasonable precautions against disclosure support a finding that privilege was maintained despite unauthorized disclosure.
