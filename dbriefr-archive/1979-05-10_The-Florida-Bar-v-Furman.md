Title: The Florida Bar v. Furman
Citation: 376 So. 2d 378 (Fla. 1979)
Court: Supreme Court of Florida
Date: May 10, 1979 (Opinion clarified Nov. 1, 1979)

Summary:
The Florida Supreme Court held that Rosemary W. Furman, operator of a "do-it-yourself divorce kit" and secretarial service, engaged in the unauthorized practice of law by preparing pleadings, advising clients, conducting intake interviews, briefing clients on courtroom procedure, and otherwise assisting litigants beyond mere clerical work. The Court permanently enjoined her from performing the proscribed activities and directed The Florida Bar to study ways to improve legal services for the indigent.

FACTS:
Respondent Furman operated Northside Secretarial Service and sold printed materials and forms (so-called "do-it-yourself divorce kits") and provided paid assistance (reported fees up to $50) in family-law matters (dissolutions and adoptions) in 1976–1977. Although Furman disclaimed being an attorney and customers admitted they did not believe she was a lawyer, she: completed intake sheets for clients who could not complete them, asked oral questions to obtain facts, typed petitions and other court papers, advised customers how and where to file, tracked cases through the clerk's office, and conducted pretrial "briefing sessions" instructing clients how to present their case and where judges sit. The referee found these services went well beyond typing and selling forms and constituted the practice of law.

PROCEDURAL HISTORY:
- Sept. 23, 1977: The Florida Bar filed an amended petition alleging Furman engaged in unauthorized practice of law.
- The case was referred to a referee (retired judge) who took evidence and filed a report finding Furman had engaged in unauthorized practice and recommending a permanent injunction and contempt adjudication.
- May 10, 1979: The Supreme Court of Florida issued the opinion finding unauthorized practice and permanently enjoining Furman from further such activities; opinion clarified Nov. 1, 1979.

ISSUES:
1. Did Furman's activities constitute the unauthorized practice of law under Florida precedent (including Brumbaugh)?
2. What activities may a non-lawyer engage in (sale/typing of forms) without crossing into the unauthorized practice of law?
3. What remedy and ancillary action (Bar study on indigent legal services) are appropriate once unauthorized practice is found?

HOLDING:
The Court held that Furman's conduct — taking facts orally, completing intake forms for illiterate or unfamiliar clients, preparing pleadings, advising on filing, and briefing clients about courtroom procedure — constituted the unauthorized practice of law. The Court permanently enjoined her from engaging in those activities and directed the Bar to study how to improve access to legal services for the indigent.

RULE OF LAW:
- Non-lawyers may sell printed legal materials and may type forms completed by clients, but they may not advise clients on remedies, assist in preparing forms in a way that affects substantive rights, answer legal questions about which forms to use, instruct how to present evidence, or otherwise provide personal legal assistance in conjunction with a paid business. (Cites Florida Bar v. Brumbaugh, 355 So. 2d 1186.)
- The court has supervisory authority to enjoin unauthorized practice and to mandate institutional responses to access-to-justice problems.

REASONING / ANALYSIS:
- The referee's factual findings (based on intake sheets, exhibits, and testimony) showed Furman's business routinely conducted intake interviews, completed forms for clients who were illiterate or unfamiliar with legal terminology, prepared petitions and related pleadings, tracked filing and progress with clerk's office, and conducted in-person pretrial briefings that instructed clients how to present testimony and where to find the judge. These activities, the Court reasoned, go beyond permissible clerical acts described in Brumbaugh and constitute legal practice because they affect substantive rights and the conduct of litigation.
- The Court acknowledged the access-to-justice concern raised by Furman and amicus but declined to use this case to reform service delivery; instead, it ordered the Bar to study and report on better methods for providing legal services to the indigent, so the access problem could be addressed separately and thoroughly.

DISPOSITION:
Respondent Rosemary W. Furman is permanently enjoined from engaging in the unauthorized practice of law as described in the opinion. The Court directed The Florida Bar to undertake a study on ways to provide effective legal services to the indigent and to file a report by January 1, 1980.

SIGNIFICANCE / NOTES:
- Furman clarifies and enforces the Brumbaugh line: sale of forms and simple typing is permitted, but oral intake, filling in forms for clients, advising on legal strategy, and preparing or correcting substantive pleadings are prohibited for non-lawyers.
- The opinion balances enforcement against unauthorized practice with recognition of access-to-justice issues by directing the Bar to study systemic solutions.
