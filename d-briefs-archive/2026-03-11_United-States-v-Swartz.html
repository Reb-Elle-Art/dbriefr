United States v. Swartz
945 F. Supp. 2d 216 (D. Mass. 2013) U.S. District Court for the District of Massachusetts May 8, 2013
Briefed by: Zoe
Facts
Defendant: Aaron Swartz, internet activist, Reddit co-founder, RSS specification co-author Charges: 13 felony counts including wire fraud and violations of the Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (CFAA) Alleged Conduct: Between September 2010 and January 2011, Swartz downloaded approximately 4.8 million academic journal articles from JSTOR using MIT's computer network. Method: • Used a Python script named "keepgrabbing.py" • Connected a laptop to MIT's network in an unlocked wiring closet • Spoofed MAC addresses to circumvent network blocks Context: JSTOR articles were behind paywalls; MIT provided free access to the network but not bulk downloading
Procedural History
Swartz was indicted by federal prosecutors in July 2011 Charges were increased to 13 felony counts in September 2012 Motion hearings held on discovery matters May 8, 2013: Court issued opinion on discovery motions (the cited opinion) January 11, 2013: Swartz died by suicide before trial Case dismissed following defendant's death
Issue(s)
This opinion addressed: 1. Whether the government must produce certain discovery materials requested by the defense 2. Whether names of MIT, JSTOR, and government employees should be disclosed in public filings 3. Whether details of MIT's computer network security should be made public
Holding
1. Discovery Materials: Granted in part — ordered disclosure of most materials sought, subject to redactions 2. Employee Names: Denied — privacy and safety interests substantially outweighed by disclosure 3. Network Security Details: Denied — MIT's computer network security details should not be made public
Rule of Law
• Discovery in criminal cases must balance public transparency against legitimate privacy and safety concerns • Government and victim employees have substantial interest in being shielded from potential retaliation • Threats and harassment against participants in criminal prosecutions can justify protective orders and redactions • Computer network security details may be withheld to prevent future security breaches
Reasoning / Rationale
The court balanced several competing interests: Employee Safety: • The court acknowledged documented threats and harassment against prosecutors known to be involved in the case • Examples cited: "threatening emails," Facebook hacking, postcard depicting prosecutor's father in guillotine • Concluded that safety interests "substantially outweighed" public interest in disclosure Network Security: • MIT's network security details withheld to prevent potential future security breaches • Rationale: Public disclosure could facilitate unauthorized access to MIT systems Discovery Compliance: • Ordered government to produce most requested materials • Permitted redactions to protect privacy and security
Concurring Opinion(s)
None. (This was a district court opinion on pretrial motions, not an appellate decision)
Dissenting Opinion(s)
None.
Analysis & Significance
This case became a flashpoint for national debate about: 1. CFAA Scope: Whether violating terms of service constitutes "unauthorized access" under the Computer Fraud and Abuse Act 2. Prosecutorial Discretion: Swartz faced up to 35 years in prison and $1 million in fines, sparking criticism about overcharging 3. Internet Activism vs. Criminal Conduct: The line between civil disobedience and federal crimes 4. Academic Access: The paywall model for academic research and information freedom Legislative Response: • Led to proposed "Aaron's Law" legislation to narrow the CFAA • Failed to pass Congress but sparked ongoing reform efforts Cultural Impact: • Galvanized the open access movement • Influenced MIT and JSTOR policy changes • Remains a touchstone in debates about prosecutorial proportionality in technology cases
Additional Notes
• Case never reached trial — all legal questions about CFAA interpretation remain unsettled in this specific context • The government's theory (that violating TOS = unauthorized access) has been rejected by some circuits but adopted by others, creating circuit split • Ortiz (U.S. Attorney) defended charging decision; criticism came from legal scholars across political spectrum • MIT conducted internal review; ultimately found no wrongdoing but revised policies on network access
