Marbury v. Madison
5 U.S. (1 Cranch) 137 (1803) | U.S. Supreme Court | 1803-02-24
Facts

William Marbury was appointed as a justice of the peace for the District of Columbia by President John Adams in the final days of his presidency. The appointment was confirmed by the Senate and the commission was signed and sealed, but not delivered before Adams left office. President Jefferson, the incoming president, instructed his Secretary of State, James Madison, not to deliver the commission. Marbury petitioned the Supreme Court for a writ of mandamus to compel Madison to deliver the commission.

Procedural History

Marbury brought his action directly to the U.S. Supreme Court under Section 13 of the Judiciary Act of 1789, which granted the Supreme Court original jurisdiction to issue writs of mandamus to federal officers. This was not an appeal from a lower court but rather an original action filed directly with the Supreme Court.

Issue(s)

1. Does Marbury have a right to the commission? 2. If he has a right, is there a legal remedy available? 3. Is a writ of mandamus the appropriate remedy? 4. Does the Supreme Court have the constitutional authority to issue the writ under Section 13 of the Judiciary Act of 1789?

Holding

The Court held that: (1) Marbury had a legal right to the commission because it was a completed act once signed and sealed; (2) the law must provide a remedy for violation of a vested legal right; (3) mandamus was the appropriate remedy; but (4) Section 13 of the Judiciary Act of 1789 was unconstitutional because it attempted to expand the Supreme Court's original jurisdiction beyond what Article III of the Constitution allows. The petition for mandamus was denied.

Rule of Law

It is emphatically the province and duty of the judicial department to say what the law is. The Constitution is superior to ordinary legislative acts, and when a law is in conflict with the Constitution, the Constitution must govern. The Supreme Court has the authority of judicial review—the power to declare acts of Congress unconstitutional when they conflict with the Constitution. Congress cannot expand the Supreme Court's original jurisdiction beyond the limits set forth in Article III, Section 2.

Reasoning / Rationale

Marshall established three principles: First, Marbury had a vested legal right to the commission because the appointment process was complete when the President signed and sealed it. Second, for every violation of a vested right, there must be a legal remedy—"the very essence of civil liberty certainly consists in the right of every individual to claim the protection of the laws." Third, the Court examined whether mandamus was the appropriate remedy and found it was. However, the crucial question was whether the Court had jurisdiction to issue it. Marshall reasoned that Article III of the Constitution defines the Supreme Court's original and appellate jurisdiction, and Congress cannot expand it. Section 13 purported to give the Court original jurisdiction to issue mandamus, but this conflicted with Article III. Therefore, the statute was unconstitutional.

Analysis & Significance

Marbury v. Madison established the principle of judicial review in the United States—the power of federal courts to declare acts of Congress unconstitutional. This is arguably the most important decision in American constitutional law, as it positioned the judiciary as a co-equal branch of government with the power to check legislative and executive actions. The case has been cited and reaffirmed countless times, including in Cooper v. Aaron (1958) where the Court stated that Marbury established "the basic principle that the federal judiciary is supreme in the exposition of the law of the Constitution."
