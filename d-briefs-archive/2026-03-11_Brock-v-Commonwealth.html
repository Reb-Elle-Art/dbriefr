Brock v. Commonwealth
Record No. 0756-94-3 (Va. Ct. App. 1995) | Court of Appeals of Virginia | December 19, 1995
Facts

Date of Incident: July 30, 1993 Location: 359-unit mini-warehouse storage complex in Staunton, Virginia, owned by Calvin VanFossen Defendant: Robert Lee Brock (appellant) Co-defendants: James Adams and James Sandy (pleaded guilty, testified for Commonwealth)

Criminal Conduct: • Brock, Adams, and Sandy broke into three storage units and stole goods (these convictions were not appealed) • Nine other units (Nos. 318, 319, 321, 322, 323, 334, 337, 338, 339) had damaged doors but no entry gained — these formed the basis of the attempted breaking and entering charges

Key Testimony: • Adams: Saw Brock at "four or five doors" away pulling on a lock with a screwdriver, saying it was an "easy one" and he "didn't have his key." Adams tried it, it didn't open, and they left. Adams could not identify which specific units Brock attempted to enter. • Sandy: Could not identify which buildings Brock entered or attempted to enter; explicitly denied knowledge of attempts on the nine specific units at issue • VanFossen: Observed damage to the nine units the next morning but could not state when he last observed them or whether damage appeared recent • Prior Incidents: Evidence showed other break-ins had occurred at the complex within approximately 30 days

Procedural History

Bench trial in Circuit Court of the City of Staunton

Brock convicted of: • 9 counts attempted breaking and entering in the nighttime (appealed) • 3 counts breaking and entering (not appealed) • 4 counts grand larceny (not appealed)

Appeal to Court of Appeals of Virginia

Disposition: Judgment reversed and dismissed

Issue(s)

Whether the evidence was sufficient to support Brock's convictions for nine counts of attempted breaking and entering in the nighttime.

Holding

Reversed and dismissed. The evidence was insufficient to prove Brock was the criminal agent who damaged the nine specific storage units.

Rule of Law

An attempt to commit a crime requires: 1. Specific intent to commit the particular crime, AND 2. An ineffectual overt act done toward its commission

Additional principles: • Intent is a state of mind evidenced only by the words or conduct of the person claimed to have entertained it • An overt act is required to prove an attempted offense because without it, there is too much uncertainty as to the accused's actual intent • Evidence must prove the defendant was the "criminal agent" who committed the alleged acts

Reasoning / Rationale

The court identified four fatal evidentiary gaps:

1. No Link to Specific Units: • While Adams testified Brock attempted to open a unit with a screwdriver, neither co-defendant could identify the nine specific units at issue • Adams described Brock being "four or five doors" away — nowhere near the nine units for which he was convicted

2. No Temporal Evidence: • VanFossen could not state when he last observed the nine units • VanFossen could not state whether the damage appeared recent • Testimony established prior break-ins occurred within 30 days

3. Co-defendants Denied Knowledge: • Both Sandy and Adams explicitly denied knowledge of Brock attempting to enter the nine specific units • Neither could identify which specific units were involved

4. Alternative Explanation: • The damage could have been caused by prior criminal activity or other unknown actors • No evidence excluded other possible causes

Motion to Strike Not Required: • Brock's closing argument challenged sufficiency of evidence • Commonwealth argued Williams v. Commonwealth barred the argument • Court cited Campbell v. Commonwealth (en banc) overruling Williams on this point • Even assuming argument was not "clearly presented," court held "the ends of justice require that appellant's convictions cannot be justified when the evidence does not prove that appellant was the criminal agent"

Analysis & Significance

Evidentiary Standard: • Reinforces requirement that attempt convictions require proof of specific intent plus overt act • Clarifies that mere proximity to crime scene is insufficient without direct evidence linking defendant to specific acts

Co-defendant Testimony Limitations: • Demonstrates limitations of accomplice testimony when witnesses cannot identify specific elements of the crime • Shows importance of temporal evidence (when damage occurred) not just that damage existed

Prosecutorial Strategy: • Case illustrates risk of overcharging based on circumstantial evidence • Nine counts of attempted breaking and entering collapsed for lack of specific evidence tying defendant to each unit

Additional Notes

Inconsistent Statements: • Commonwealth argued Brock made inconsistent statements at trial vs. sentencing • Court distinguished Beavers v. Commonwealth — found no relevant inconsistency here • Brock consistently denied involvement with the nine specific units

Procedural Note: • Case was decided on sufficiency of evidence grounds — fundamental issue of proof, not technical procedural bar • Court reached merits despite motion to strike issue because "ends of justice" required it

Practice Pointer: • Prosecutors should ensure witnesses can identify specific elements of each charged offense • Temporal evidence (when acts occurred) is as important as proof that acts occurred
